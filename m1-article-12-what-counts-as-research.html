What Counts as Research?

Research isn't Googling something and hoping the first link is right. It's asking good questions, finding reliable sources, and figuring out what's actually true — and AI can help you get much better at all three.

With millions of websites, social media posts, videos, and AI tools all competing for your attention, figuring out what counts as "real research" can feel genuinely overwhelming. You open your laptop with a topic, start searching, and immediately get buried.

Research is the process of asking good questions, finding reliable information, and deciding what's actually true. That sounds simple — but it's a skill most students are never explicitly taught. Let's fix that.

Why Research Is Harder Than It Looks

Most students aren't taught how to research — they're just told to "go find sources." So you open your browser and suddenly you're dealing with all of this:

💻
Random blogs with opinions disguised as facts — written to sound authoritative but with no real evidence behind them
🤖
AI answers that sound convincing but might be wrong — AI can generate confident-sounding information that is completely made up
🔎
Search results optimized for clicks, not accuracy — the top result isn't necessarily the most reliable or relevant one
⁉️
Confusing academic language — legitimate research is often written for experts, making it hard for students to extract what they actually need
📑
Long articles with unclear main points — even good sources can be dense and hard to navigate without a system for pulling out what matters

It's not your fault if research feels hard. Finding good information takes skill — and that skill starts with understanding the types of sources you're dealing with.

The goal of research isn't to find information. It's to find reliable information — and know the difference between the two.

Primary vs. Secondary Sources

Every source you encounter falls into one of two categories — and knowing which you're looking at (and which you need) is one of the most foundational research skills there is:

Type 1
📄
Primary Sources
Original, first-hand information — the raw material of research
Interviews and firsthand accounts
Original experiments and data
Historical documents and letters
Surveys and raw census data
Original literary or artistic works
Type 2
📚
Secondary Sources
Someone else's analysis or interpretation of primary information
Textbooks and academic reviews
News articles and journalism
Documentaries and explainer videos
Biographies and historical analyses
Literary criticism and book reviews

Both types are valuable — and the right one depends on your assignment. AI can help you figure out which kind you actually need before you waste time searching for the wrong thing:

💬 Example Prompt
You type:
"I'm writing a paper on the impacts of climate change on coastal communities. Do I need primary or secondary sources — and what's the difference for this topic specifically?"
✅ What AI Helps You Understand
AI will explain that for this topic, primary sources might include climate data from NOAA, scientific studies measuring sea level rise, or interviews with affected residents — while secondary sources would include news coverage, textbook explanations of climate science, or documentary analyses. It helps you understand what you're looking for before you go looking for it.

Reliable vs. Unreliable Sources

Not everything online deserves your trust — and the difference between a reliable and unreliable source isn't always obvious. Click each category to see how to spot them:

✅
Signs of a Reliable Source
+

Reliable sources share a few key characteristics — not because they're "official," but because they're transparent about where their information comes from and how they arrived at their conclusions.

✅ Look For
✔Named authors with verifiable credentials
✔Citations and references to other sources
✔Published recently or clearly dated
✔From institutions, universities, or known publications
✔Presents evidence, not just conclusions
✔Acknowledges uncertainty or limitations
🚩 Watch Out For
✘No author listed or anonymous
✘No citations or "trust me" language
✘Undated or very old for a fast-moving topic
✘Website you've never heard of with no "About" page
✘Makes dramatic claims without evidence
✘Strongly one-sided with no counterargument
💬 Try This Prompt
"Here are two sources I found about vaccine safety. Can you compare them and tell me which seems more credible and why?" [paste both URLs or summaries]
⚠️
A Special Warning: AI as a Source
+

Here's something important that often gets overlooked: AI itself is not a reliable research source. AI tools like ChatGPT can generate information that sounds completely accurate but is actually fabricated — this is called "hallucination." AI is trained on patterns in text, not on verified facts, which means it can confidently state things that are simply wrong.

✅ Good AI Research Uses
✔Helping you form better research questions
✔Explaining confusing concepts simply
✔Comparing sources you already found
✔Suggesting what types of sources to look for
🚩 Don't Use AI For
✘Citing as a primary or secondary source
✘Verifying facts without cross-checking
✘Statistics or specific data claims
✘Quotes from real people or documents

Always verify anything AI tells you against a real, citable source before including it in your work.

Asking Better Research Questions

Research always starts with a question — and the quality of your question determines the quality of your results. Most students start with questions that are too broad to be useful. Here's the difference:

❌ Weak Research Questions
→"Tell me everything about space."
→"What is climate change?"
→"Why is history important?"
→"How does technology work?"
✅ Strong Research Questions
→"What challenges do astronauts face during missions longer than six months?"
→"How does rising sea level specifically affect low-income coastal communities?"
→"What role did propaganda play in shaping public opinion during WWII?"
→"How does social media algorithm design influence teen mental health?"

Good research questions share four qualities that make them actually useful:

1
Clear

You can read it and immediately know exactly what you're asking — no ambiguity about what counts as a good answer.

2
Specific

Narrow enough that you can actually find and evaluate sources that address it directly, rather than drowning in everything ever written on a broad topic.

3
Open-Ended

Doesn't have a simple yes/no answer. Invites analysis, comparison, or explanation — which is what research actually produces.

4
Focused on Real Information

Grounded in verifiable facts or evidence — not just opinions or hypotheticals that can't be researched in a meaningful way.

💬 Let AI Help Sharpen Your Question
You type:
"My topic is social media and teens. Help me turn this into a specific, focused research question that would lead to good sources."
✅ What AI Helps You Do
AI can generate several sharper versions — like "How does Instagram's algorithm affect body image in girls aged 13–17?" or "What does current research say about social media use and sleep quality in teenagers?" — giving you a concrete starting point instead of a vague topic drift.
🔍

Research Is a Skill — Not Just a Task

Most students treat research as something you do once to fill a bibliography. But it's actually a thinking skill — one that gets sharper every time you practice asking better questions, evaluating sources carefully, and deciding what to trust. AI can help you build that skill faster, but the critical thinking part always has to come from you.

Why This Matters Beyond School

Being able to research well isn't just about getting good grades. These skills shape how you move through the world:

🧠
Critical Thinking

You question claims instead of accepting them at face value — whether it's a news headline, a product claim, or a political statement.

🚫
Spotting Misinformation

You recognize emotional manipulation, missing citations, and one-sided framing — the hallmarks of unreliable information online.

⚖️
Making Informed Decisions

From choosing a college to evaluating a news story to making health decisions — good research skills help you make choices based on evidence, not noise.

💼
Career Advantage

In virtually every profession, the ability to find, evaluate, and synthesize reliable information is a core skill that sets strong performers apart.

🌍
Understanding the World

The more accurately you can understand what's actually happening — and why — the better equipped you are to participate meaningfully in the world around you.

🗣️
Arguing With Evidence

Good research makes you a more persuasive communicator — because you can back your positions with real evidence instead of just strong opinions.

In a world full of confident-sounding misinformation, the ability to find and evaluate reliable sources isn't a nice-to-have. It's one of the most important skills you can develop.

📌 Article Summary

What You Just Learned

Research is the process of asking good questions, finding reliable information, and evaluating what's actually true — not just Googling something and citing the first result. It's a skill that requires practice.

Primary sources are original, first-hand information. Secondary sources are analyses or interpretations of primary information. Both matter — and knowing which you need before you search saves significant time and effort.

Reliable sources cite their evidence, name their authors, and come from credible institutions. AI itself is not a reliable research source — it can confidently generate inaccurate information, so always verify AI-provided facts elsewhere.

Good research questions are clear, specific, open-ended, and grounded in verifiable information. AI can help you sharpen a broad topic into a focused, useful question — and compare sources you've already found for credibility.
