Module 1 · Introduction to AI

Using Technology the Right Way

AI is powerful — but power without responsibility causes problems. Here's how to think about fairness, privacy, honesty, and accountability when using AI tools.

AI can help us write essays faster, practice math problems, or recommend the perfect playlist. It's genuinely useful — and it's only going to become more useful as it improves. But just because we can use AI a certain way doesn't mean we should.

This is where ethics comes in. Ethics is the study of right and wrong — it's how we decide which choices are fair, responsible, and honest. With AI becoming part of school, work, and daily life, thinking ethically about how we use it isn't optional. It's one of the most important skills you can develop.

Why Ethics Matters in AI

To understand why this matters, consider what happens when AI gets used without ethical thinking. These aren't hypothetical scenarios — versions of each have already happened in the real world:

🎓
A teacher relies too heavily on an AI grading system that makes mistakes — unfairly penalizing students for things they actually got right, with no human review to catch the errors.
🏢
A company uses AI to collect private information about people — their location, browsing habits, even voice recordings — without clearly asking for permission or explaining how it's used.
📝
A student uses AI to complete all their assignments without engaging with the material — graduating without actually learning the skills the coursework was designed to build.

None of these outcomes required malicious intent. They happened because the people involved didn't pause to ask: Is this fair? Is this honest? Who is responsible if something goes wrong? That's exactly what ethical thinking helps you do.

Without ethical thinking, AI could cause more problems than it solves — not because the technology is evil, but because the people using it didn't stop to ask the right questions.

The Four Core AI Ethics Issues

There are four big ethical questions that come up again and again when AI is involved. Click each one to understand what it means and why it matters:

⚖️
Fairness
+

AI systems can be biased — sometimes in ways that are hard to detect. If an AI is trained mostly on data from one group of people, it may not work as well for others. This can lead to unfair outcomes in hiring, lending, medical care, and even criminal justice.

🔑 Key Question: Is this AI tool fair for everyone it affects — not just the people who designed or paid for it?
🔍 Real Example
Some facial recognition systems have been shown to be significantly less accurate at identifying women and people with darker skin tones — because the training data was overwhelmingly made up of white male faces. The result: technology that works well for some people and poorly for others.
🔒
Privacy
+

AI often needs data to work — your clicks, searches, location, purchase history, or even voice recordings. The ethical question is: does the person whose data is being used know about it, understand it, and agree to it? Collecting data without clear consent is one of the most common ethical problems in tech today.

🔑 Key Question: Does the person whose data is being collected know exactly what's being collected, how it's being used, and who it's being shared with?
🔍 Real Example
Many free apps collect detailed behavioral data — how long you pause on a post, what you search for, where you go — and sell it to advertisers. Most users have no idea the extent of this collection even though it's technically in the terms of service they clicked through.
✋
Honesty
+

Using AI raises real questions about honesty and transparency. If AI helped you write something, should you say so? If a company uses AI to make decisions that affect you, should they disclose it? Honesty in AI use is about being upfront — both about when you used it and about what the AI produced.

🔑 Key Question: Am I being transparent about when and how I used AI — and would I be comfortable if others knew exactly how I used it?
🔍 In School
Is it okay to use AI to help brainstorm ideas for an essay? Probably. Is it okay to have AI write the whole essay and submit it as your own work? That's dishonest — and it also means you didn't do the learning the assignment was designed to create. The line is blurry, which is why being transparent with your teacher matters.
🙋
Responsibility
+

AI doesn't make moral decisions — people do. When an AI system makes a mistake that harms someone, a human is still responsible for that outcome. Whether it's the person who built the system, the company that deployed it, or the person who used it — accountability doesn't disappear just because a machine was involved.

🔑 Key Question: If this AI makes a mistake, who is responsible for the consequences — and am I comfortable taking that responsibility?
🔍 Real Example
When a self-driving car causes an accident, who is liable? The passenger? The company that built the AI? The software engineers? These are real legal and ethical questions being debated right now — and they don't have simple answers yet.

Who Is Responsible When AI Gets It Wrong?

Responsibility for AI outcomes is shared across multiple groups — it's rarely just one person's fault. Here's how the chain typically works:

🏗️
The Builders

The engineers and companies that design AI systems are responsible for building them fairly, testing for bias, and being transparent about limitations. If the system is fundamentally flawed, this is where accountability starts.

🏢
The Deployers

Businesses and organizations that use AI tools are responsible for applying them appropriately. Using an AI tool in a context it wasn't designed for — or ignoring its known flaws — is an ethical failure on the deployer's part.

🧑‍💻
The Users

Individual users are responsible for how they apply AI tools in their own lives. Using AI to deceive others, bypass learning, or make high-stakes decisions without human judgment is an ethical failure at the user level.

In most real situations, responsibility is shared across all three. That's what makes AI ethics complicated — and why it requires everyone, not just engineers, to think carefully about how they engage with these tools.

🌍

The Bigger Picture

Ethics in AI isn't just a school issue — it's shaping the future. The choices people make today about how to build, deploy, and use AI will determine whether this technology creates fair opportunities for everyone or deepens existing inequalities. Your generation will inherit both the benefits and the consequences of those choices — which is exactly why understanding this now matters.

📌 Article Summary

What You Just Learned

Ethics means thinking about right and wrong. In AI, ethical thinking asks: Is this fair? Is this honest? Does this respect people's privacy? Who is responsible if something goes wrong?

The four core AI ethics issues are fairness (AI can be biased), privacy (AI collects data that may not be fully understood by users), honesty (transparency about when and how AI was used), and responsibility (humans are accountable for AI outcomes).

Responsibility for AI is shared — builders, deployers, and users all play a role. When AI causes harm, it's rarely just one person's fault, which is why everyone who interacts with AI needs to think critically about how they use it.

The choices your generation makes about AI will shape the future. Using AI wisely, fairly, and honestly isn't just the right thing to do — it's how you help make sure AI benefits everyone, not just some people.
