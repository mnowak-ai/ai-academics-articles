Finding Real Sources
(Not Fake Ones)

AI is powerful — but it can invent citations, make up authors, and hallucinate studies that don't exist. Here's how to stay in control and find sources you can actually trust.

The Problem with Fake Citations

AI doesn't lie on purpose — but it guesses based on patterns. Sometimes that guessing leads to invented books, fake article titles, and non-existent researchers. It's called hallucination, and it can happen to anyone who trusts AI output without checking.

Here are four types of fake content AI can accidentally generate:

📚
Books that don't exist
📑
Articles never published
📖
Studies with made-up data
⁉️
Authors who aren't real people

In school — and in life — submitting a fake source can seriously hurt your credibility. The good news? You can avoid this entirely by using AI the right way.

AI Should Help You Identify Sources, Not Create Them

There's a clear line between what AI is good at and what puts you at risk. Here's the rule:

✅ Safe to Ask AI
✓
"What types of sources should I look for on this topic?"
✓
"What source categories are most credible for science research?"
✓
"Where could I find reliable government data on unemployment?"
✓
"Help me understand what makes a source trustworthy."
❌ Risky to Ask AI
✗
"Give me real articles with working links on this topic."
✗
"Find me a study that proves X."
✗
"List 5 books I can cite about climate change."
✗
"Write a bibliography for my paper."

The safe approach: ask AI for source categories and search directions, then verify everything yourself.

💡 Try This Prompt
You type:
"What types of sources should I look for when researching the effects of social media on teen mental health?"

AI might suggest source categories like:

1
Peer-reviewed psychology journals (e.g., JAMA, The Lancet)
2
Government health organization reports (CDC, NIH)
3
Reputable news outlets covering mental health research
4
University research centers and published white papers

Notice the difference: AI is pointing you toward a type of source — not handing you a specific title to copy-paste. Now it's your job to actually find those sources yourself.

Tools to Find Real Sources

Once you know what category of source you need, head to one of these trusted places to find the real thing:

🎓
Google Scholar
📚
School Library Databases
🏛️
Government Websites (.gov)
🏫
Museum or University Sites
📰
Reputable Newspapers & News Sites
🔬
Science Organizations

You can still use AI here too — just frame the question as guidance, not sourcing: "Where could I find reliable sources on ______?" Let it point you to the right neighborhoods, then go explore yourself.

Spotting Real vs. Fake Sources

Once you find a source, you need to verify it before using it. A real, trustworthy source will have these six things. Click each one to check it off:

✅ The Real Source Checklist
An author's name you can look up
A publication date
A publisher or institution behind it
A working link or findable location
Evidence, data, or citations within it
A title you can independently search and confirm

The Quick Fake-Source Test

Before using any source — especially one AI mentioned — ask yourself:

Can I find this source on the internet by searching the title and author name?

If the answer is no… it's probably fake. Don't use it.

AI makes research easier — but you are the one who locates the real materials.

What AI Can Do Once You Find a Source

Here's the good news: once you have a real, verified source in front of you, AI becomes incredibly useful. You can paste in the text and ask it to:

📝
Summarize it
🔍
Explain confusing sections
🎯
Clarify its purpose
⚖️
Analyze its credibility
🔗
Compare it to other sources

Think of it this way: you bring the real sources, AI brings the analysis. Together you make a research team that's fast, thorough, and trustworthy.

📌 What You Learned
AI can hallucinate fake citations, authors, and studies — it guesses based on patterns, not truth.
Ask AI for source categories and search directions — never ask it to hand you specific links or citations.
Find real sources in trusted places: Google Scholar, library databases, .gov sites, and reputable news outlets.
A real source has an author, date, publisher, working link, evidence, and a title you can independently verify.
Once you've found a real source, AI is a powerful tool for summarizing, analyzing, and comparing it.
